> Your company does not need forward-deployed engineers.
> It needs forward-deployed bloodhounds.
> I know that sounds ridiculous. I also think it's closer to the truth than what most of the market is saying right now.
> There's a lot of talk about forward-deployed engineers becoming the most important role in AI. It's accelerating quickly. The story is usually framed as a technical one. Companies need brilliant engineers who can go into the field, understand the customer, wire up systems, deploy agents, and turn AI into something that actually works.
> That is directionally right. But it quietly assumes the hard part is the build.
> My argument is that the hard part is knowing what the right things to build actually are.
> The market is selling AI implementation as an engineering problem. After spending real time inside real companies, I'm increasingly convinced it's a seeing problem with engineering bolted on the back.
> You absolutely need world-class engineers, they're more valuable now than they've ever been. I'm very, very bullish on engineers. And you NEED a team of cracked out engineers to implement AI correctly in the enterprise.
> But even the best engineering team in the world is only as useful as the problem you point them at. Put a brilliant engineer in front of a broken process and they will fix it, whether or not fixing it actually moves the business. So you end up with brilliant people shipping expensive solutions to things that never mattered.
> A company might have a thousand broken processes. Most of them don't matter. A handful do. The entire return on an AI implementation depends on whether someone can walk the floor, ignore the noise, and find the few that actually move the business. Miss that, and you've shipped beautiful agents against problems nobody needed solved.
> That's not an engineering skill. That's a completely different profile.
> So what are the qualities of this "bloodhound" that companies actually need?
> The people who will succeed in these environments are obsessive operators (who sometimes will in fact be engineers!) with a pathological intolerance for bullshit. They keep asking "why?" long after everyone else is socially ready to move on. They notice contradictions instantly. They get irrationally bothered by workflows that don't make sense. They ignore org charts, status games, and political theatre because they instinctively understand that the documented process and the real process are almost never the same thing.
> They peg everything against real business constraints.
> They're wired to find the gap between the story a company tells about itself and the reality that exists.
> A lot of AI implementation firms are selling the same image. Sharp technical consultants parachuting into companies with clean architecture diagrams, perfect systems thinking, and a lot of buzzword language about transformation.
> That's not who I'd bet on.
> I'd bet on the obsessive operator who notices things that don't add up and refuses to move on until they understand why. The person who asks "why?" until the room gets uncomfortable. The person who notices that the SOP says one thing, the CRM says another, the dashboard says another, and later finds out the woman who has worked there for 20 years quietly knows the real answer.
> That person is dangerous in the best possible way.
> Because most businesses do not run the way they describe themselves. And this is exactly why I think the title "forward-deployed engineer" is incomplete.
> Once again, I'm not diminishing the need for engineers. We need them now more than ever, and I am very, very bullish on engineering talent. If your implementation firm doesn't have serious engineering talent behind the work, you need to walk away.
